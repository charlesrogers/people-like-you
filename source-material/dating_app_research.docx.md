**The Science of Chemistry in Dating**  
Research Synthesis for Dating App Design

Thesis Confirmation, Missing Mechanisms, and Matching Vectors

Prepared: March 2026

*Working Document*

# **Executive Summary**

This document synthesizes the academic research on romantic chemistry, interpersonal attraction, and mate selection to inform the design of a dating app built around a core thesis: chemistry is not a fixed trait that can be predicted by algorithms; it is an emergent experience that arises from interaction design. Current dating apps optimize for sorting (swipe yes/no as fast as possible), but the actual job users are hiring for is falling for someone. These are radically different jobs requiring radically different mechanisms.

The research confirms three proposed mechanisms (Discovery, Investment, and Chemistry Creation) and identifies four additional mechanisms that are absent from the current framework: Self-Expansion, I-Sharing, Arousal Transfer through Shared Novel Experience, and Comfort/Psychological Safety.

# **Part 1: The Core Thesis**

**Thesis:** There are many people you could be a great match for, but you cannot find them. And when you do meet them, you do not have the chemistry required. This is because (a) it is hard to find the intersection point of admiration where you discover something genuinely remarkable about someone, and (b) people cannot efficiently signal their actual value, so they get filtered out before attraction can develop.

**The three core jobs of a dating app:**

* **Discovery** — Finding people you would actually want to date. Current apps solve this with proximity \+ photos \+ basic filters, which is extremely low-signal.

* **Investment** — Getting people to put in real effort to know each other. The dominant dynamic of low-effort swiping creates a race to the bottom where nobody invests because the perceived cost of moving on is zero.

* **Chemistry Creation** — Lowering the barrier to chemistry by solving the admiration gap (current formats do not surface someone's unique genius) and the credibility gap (people cannot efficiently signal their accomplishments, depth, and competence).

# **Part 2: Evidence Confirming the Thesis**

## **Confirmation 1: Trait-Matching Cannot Predict Chemistry**

The strongest validation of the thesis comes from Joel, Eastwick, and Finkel's speed-dating study using machine learning. Their algorithm could predict who would be generally desirable (some people were liked by many), but it could not predict which two specific people would experience mutual attraction. As Joel stated: "A relationship is more than the sum of its parts. There is a shared experience that happens when you meet someone that can't be predicted beforehand." Eastwick further noted that romantic desire may function more like an earthquake — a dynamic, chaotic process — than a chemical reaction involving the right combination of traits. \[1\]

This finding was corroborated by Finkel, Eastwick, Karney, Reis, and Sprecher (2012), whose comprehensive review for the Association for Psychological Science concluded that despite dating sites' claims of "scientific algorithms," there is little evidence these can predict chemistry between two people. \[2\]

**Implication:** Every swipe-based app is built on the premise that you can filter your way to chemistry. The peer-reviewed evidence says this premise is fundamentally wrong.

## **Confirmation 2: The Admiration Gap Maps to the \#1 Element of Chemistry**

Liepmann, Tu, and Muise (2025) conducted a content analysis of 200 people's definitions of romantic chemistry, identifying nine distinct categories ranked by frequency: positive interaction, mutuality, comfort, compatibility, similarity, unexplainable spark, sexual attraction, intense fixation, and physiological response. \[3\]

The most frequently cited element — positive interaction — was defined as positive feelings, a sense of connection, and enjoyment of time together. Mutuality (the second most common) included reciprocity and responsiveness. Sexual attraction ranked only sixth, and physiological response ranked last. \[3\]

**Implication:** People do not fall for profiles. They fall for the experience of feeling genuinely seen and appreciated by another person. Profiles cannot generate positive interaction — they can only generate evaluation. The thesis insight that apps fail to surface someone's "unique genius" is essentially the observation that current dating app design cannot produce the top two drivers of chemistry.

## **Confirmation 3: Structured Investment Creates Closeness**

Arthur Aron's 36 Questions research (1997) demonstrated that structured, escalating, reciprocal self-disclosure between strangers produced strong feelings of closeness. Most pairs emerged from the 45-minute session with highly positive feelings for each other; one couple from the original experiment eventually married. \[4\]

The mechanism is graduated vulnerability. As one participant in a later replication noted, "Because the level of vulnerability increased gradually, I didn't notice we had entered intimate territory until we were already there." Critically, research has shown that relationships can falter when people disclose too much too early. The 36 questions provide a structured way to manage disclosure pacing. \[5\]

**Implication:** The investment mechanism is validated. People need scaffolding to go deeper; they will not do it on their own through free-form texting. A dating app should design the disclosure process rather than leaving it to chance.

## **Confirmation 4: The Credibility Gap Maps to Mate-Value Signaling**

The evolutionary psychology literature establishes that mate-value signals include physical and behavioral traits that advertise desirability, encompassing genetic quality, social status, personality, and numerous contextual variables (Wagstaff, 2022). \[6\] However, dating app profiles surface only a tiny fraction of these signals (mainly photos), while real-world credibility markers like competence, leadership, creative ability, and social proof remain invisible.

Speed-dating research by Eastwick and Finkel (2008) confirmed that participants' stated ideal preferences, assessed before the event, failed to predict what actually inspired their desire during face-to-face interactions. \[7\] People think they know what they want, but when they actually interact, different attributes matter.

**Implication:** The credibility gap is real. People have remarkable qualities that current dating formats fail to surface, leading to filtering decisions based on the least predictive signals.

# **Part 3: Missing Mechanisms**

The research identifies four additional mechanisms that are absent from the current framework but strongly supported by the evidence.

## **Missing Mechanism 1: Self-Expansion**

Aron and Aron's Self-Expansion Model (1986, updated through 2025\) proposes that humans have a fundamental motivation to grow and expand the self, and that romantic relationships are the primary vehicle for this expansion. When people enter a relationship, they literally incorporate aspects of their partner — resources, perspectives, identities — into their own self-concept. \[8\]

Empirical evidence across multiple studies shows that when couples report more self-expansion in their relationship, they report higher satisfaction, higher sexual desire, and are more likely to maintain the passionate aspects of the relationship (Muise et al., 2019; Graham & Harf, 2015; Goss et al., 2022). \[9\]

A critical finding for dating app design: when a relationship was likely to develop, men in particular were more attracted to dissimilar partners because they offered more opportunity for self-expansion. Similarity serves primarily as a signal that a relationship is likely to develop; once that is established, dissimilarity becomes attractive because it offers novelty and growth. \[8\]

**Design Implication:** Discovery should not just match people on similarity. It should surface how someone could expand your world — what novel perspectives, skills, and experiences they bring. "This person does something you have never encountered" may be a more powerful attraction signal than "this person likes the same things as you."

## **Missing Mechanism 2: Shared Novel Experience and Arousal Transfer**

Aron, Norman, Aron, McKenna, and Heyman (2000) demonstrated across five studies that shared participation in novel and arousing activities increased experienced relationship quality compared to mundane activities. Even brief (7-minute) novel tasks together boosted satisfaction, with the effect mediated by how exciting versus boring the relationship was perceived to be. \[10\]

The foundational evidence comes from Dutton and Aron's suspension bridge study (1974), which showed that men who crossed a fear-inducing suspension bridge were significantly more likely to contact an attractive female experimenter afterward, and produced more romantic/sexual content in story-writing tasks, compared to men who crossed a safe bridge. The physiological arousal from the bridge was misattributed to romantic attraction. \[11\]

Subsequent research by White, Fishbein, and Rutstein (1981) confirmed that regardless of the polarity of the arousal stimulus (positive or negative), participants in an aroused state found attractive targets more attractive than unaroused participants did. \[12\]

**Design Implication:** The first interaction between two people should not be coffee. It should be a shared novel experience — something slightly challenging, slightly exciting, slightly outside both people's comfort zones. The app should actively design or suggest these contexts.

## **Missing Mechanism 3: I-Sharing (Shared Subjective Experience)**

Pinel and colleagues (2006, 2012, 2018\) introduced the construct of I-sharing: the belief that one shares an identical, in-the-moment subjective experience with another person. This is distinct from objective similarity (having things in common) and represents a unique form of interpersonal connection. \[13\]

Across multiple studies, Pinel demonstrated that I-sharing increases liking even for objectively dissimilar others. Participants preferred similar people, but only when that person also I-shared with them. Remarkably, I-sharing with a dissimilar other produced the same increase in liking as discovering objective similarities with a non-I-sharer. The effect was powerful enough to promote liking across gender, sexual orientation, and racial boundaries. \[14\]

The mechanism operates through existential connection. People who experience higher existential isolation (the feeling of being alone in their subjective experience) show an especially strong preference for I-sharers. I-sharing also fosters prosocial behaviors: people who I-shared with others were more generous, more helpful, and even demonstrated better compromise in cohabiting relationships. \[15\]

**Design Implication:** The app should create moments where two people can discover they are experiencing the same thing at the same time — reacting the same way to a stimulus, laughing at the same moment, being struck by the same observation. This is the "click" that people describe but cannot explain, and it is distinct from having the same values or interests.

## **Missing Mechanism 4: Comfort and Psychological Safety**

The Liepmann et al. (2025) study found that comfort was the third most frequently cited element of romantic chemistry, defined as feeling at ease, authentic, experiencing easy communication, and an absence of negativity. \[3\]

Current dating apps create the opposite of comfort. Research shows that dating app users experience frequent social evaluation and potential rejection, with those having lower success reporting decreased self-esteem and increased feelings of exclusion over time (Strubel & Petrie, 2017). Over 78% of dating app users report exhaustion from the process, and choice overload research shows that people who viewed more profiles felt less satisfied and put less effort into their first messages (Iyengar & Lepper, 2000, applied to dating contexts). \[16\]

The emotional costs of rejection in online environments may be even more severe than in face-to-face interactions, and the accelerated pace of online dating allows little time to emotionally process rejection before users are prompted to evaluate the next profile (Ellison et al., 2006; Strubel & Petrie, 2017). \[17\]

**Design Implication:** The app must actively reduce the sense of being evaluated. This may include delaying or de-emphasizing photos, removing the binary swipe mechanic, creating interactions focused on shared tasks rather than sizing each other up, and introducing people through contexts where they can be authentic rather than performing datability.

# **Part 4: The Nine Elements of Romantic Chemistry**

For reference, the complete nine-element framework from Liepmann et al. (2025), ranked by frequency of citation: \[3\]

* **Positive Interaction** — Positive feelings, sense of connection or attraction, enjoyment of time together.

* **Mutuality** — Reciprocity and responsiveness; both people are equally interested and invested.

* **Comfort** — Feeling at ease, authentic, easy communication, absence of negativity.

* **Compatibility** — Shared goals, aligned life direction, complementary personalities.

* **Similarity** — Shared interests, values, backgrounds, or worldviews.

* **Unexplainable Spark** — Magical or transcendent quality that defies rational description.

* **Sexual Attraction** — Physical or sexual chemistry and desire.

* **Intense Fixation** — Preoccupation, obsessive thinking, inability to stop thinking about the person.

* **Physiological Response** — Butterflies, racing heart, physical sensations of excitement.

These nine elements corresponded in part to the Interpersonal Chemistry Model (Reis, Regan, & Lyubomirsky, 2022), which centers on "moments of connection" — interactions where goals, feelings, needs, or wishes are expressed and responded to in a supportive way. Chemistry emerges through repeated back-and-forth exchanges where synchronicity is high. \[18\]

# **Part 5: Proposed Matching Vectors**

Based on the research synthesis, the following vectors represent the dimensions along which an LLM-powered matchmaker should evaluate and present potential matches.

## **Tier 1: Self-Expansion Vectors**

*What makes someone interesting to this specific person*

### **Novel Expertise or World**

Person B has expertise or experiences in a domain that Person A has never explored but has latent interest in, based on their existing fascination patterns. The LLM identifies on-ramps: where someone's current interests create a bridge to being genuinely intrigued by someone else's domain. Research basis: Aron & Aron's self-expansion model showing that novel perspectives and resources drive attraction. \[8\]

### **Perspective Gap**

Places where life experiences diverge in ways that would be enriching rather than alienating: different cultures, radically different career paths, or having solved similar life problems in different ways. Research basis: Self-expansion studies showing dissimilar partners are preferred when relationship formation is likely. \[8\]

### **Growth Trajectory Alignment**

Not where people are, but where they are headed. Two people actively becoming something have a different energy than two people who are static. Research basis: The self-expansion model's emphasis on ongoing growth motivation as a driver of relationship satisfaction. \[9\]

## **Tier 2: I-Sharing Vectors**

*What makes two people likely to "click" in real-time interaction*

### **Humor Signature**

Not "enjoys comedy" but specifically what makes them laugh: absurdist, observational, dark, wordplay, situational. Two people who laugh at the same things will I-share constantly in daily life. Research basis: Pinel's I-sharing research showing shared subjective reactions create connection more powerful than objective similarity. \[13\] \[14\]

### **Aesthetic and Sensory Resonance**

What moves them. What they notice in a room. What kind of beauty stops them. If two people both get chills from the same type of music or both notice the same kind of detail in their environment, that creates I-sharing fuel. Research basis: I-sharing studies showing that shared in-the-moment reactions to stimuli create existential connection. \[15\]

### **Emotional Processing Style**

How they experience and narrate their inner world. Some people process through talking, others through doing. Some lead with logic and arrive at emotion, others the reverse. Matching on how people experience things, not just what they experience. Research basis: The Interpersonal Chemistry Model's emphasis on behavioral synchrony and shared identity perception. \[18\]

## **Tier 3: Admiration Vectors**

*What makes someone see the other as high-value*

### **Demonstrated Competence**

Not credentials but stories of mastery. "She taught herself to weld to build furniture for her apartment" activates admiration differently than "Engineer at Google." The LLM elicits these stories during onboarding and surfaces them selectively based on what would resonate with a specific person. Research basis: Mate-value signaling research showing competence displays function as evolved signals of quality. \[6\]

### **Values-in-Action**

Not stated values but evidence of values being lived. Specific, observable, credible demonstrations: "He coaches his daughter's soccer team and the parents say he is the most patient person they have ever seen." Research basis: Liepmann et al.'s finding that mutuality and positive interaction (behavioral demonstrations) outrank stated compatibility. \[3\]

### **Social Proof Narrative**

Friend recommendations that surface non-obvious qualities: "The thing about Marcus is he remembers everything you tell him, and three months later he will reference something you said in passing." This leverages the mate-choice copying effect documented across species. Research basis: Little et al. (2011) on social learning in human mate preferences showing that information from others' choices significantly influences attractiveness judgments. \[19\]

## **Tier 4: Stability and Security Vectors**

*What makes someone feel safe enough to be authentic*

### **Attachment Compatibility**

Identifying attachment style combinations that will not trigger each other's worst patterns. Assessed through behavioral questions rather than clinical labels. Research basis: Bowlby's attachment theory and its application to adult romantic relationships, showing that secure attachment fosters healthier communication and relationship satisfaction. \[20\]

### **Life Infrastructure Alignment**

The pragmatic factors that become dealbreakers if misaligned but do not create chemistry on their own: children, geography, religion, lifestyle pace. These are filters (eliminating bad fits) not matchers (creating good ones). Research basis: Evolutionary mate selection literature confirming these as threshold requirements. \[7\]

### **Communication Style**

Direct versus indirect communication, processing speed, and approach to conflict. Two people who can navigate disagreement well together have a better long-term shot than two people who agree on everything but cannot fight constructively. Research basis: Reis et al. on perceived partner responsiveness as a foundation for relationship well-being. \[18\] \[21\]

# **Part 6: The LLM Matchmaker Pitch Concept**

Rather than presenting a person as a bundle of attributes to be evaluated, the app presents a relationship hypothesis — a narrative about why two specific people might have something meaningful. This shifts the psychological frame from evaluation to curiosity.

## **Example Pitch**

*"You both came to your careers through unconventional paths — you dropped out of law school to start a business, she left nursing to become a UX researcher. You both know what it feels like to bet on yourself when everyone said you were crazy. She is also deeply into trail running, and given your obsession with pushing yourself in competitive sports, you would probably find her race stories fascinating. Her best friend describes her as 'the person who asks the question nobody else is brave enough to ask' — which sounds like it would either drive you crazy or be exactly the kind of person who keeps you sharp."*

## **Why This Works**

* **Self-expansion signal:** Surfaces how the other person could expand your world, not just mirror it.

* **Admiration activation:** Highlights specific, credible demonstrations of character rather than generic attributes.

* **Curiosity over evaluation:** Frames the other person as a full human with a story, creating pull rather than push.

* **Social proof:** Integrates friend-sourced observations that carry more credibility than self-report.

* **Comfort priming:** Humor and specificity reduce the sense of encountering a stranger and increase the sense of encountering a person.

## **Feedback Loop Design**

Asking "why did you not like this lead" produces garbage data — people cite surface-level dealbreakers that are not actually dealbreakers. The speed-dating research proved that stated preferences do not predict actual behavior. \[1\] \[7\] Alternative approaches:

* **Post-interaction surprise question:** After the first real interaction (not after seeing the pitch), ask "What surprised you about this person?" Surprises reveal where the match hypothesis was right or wrong.

* **Behavioral over stated signals:** Track who users actually spend time engaging with versus who they said they wanted. The gap between stated and revealed preferences is the training data.

* **Forward-looking framing:** Instead of "why did you not like them," ask "would you want to do \[specific shared activity\] with this person?" This measures interest without triggering the evaluation/rejection frame.

# **Part 7: Updated Architecture**

Based on the research synthesis, the complete mechanism framework expands from three to five:

**FIND (Discovery)** — Surface self-expansion potential and unique genius, not just similarity and attractiveness.

**INVEST (Effort)** — Structured, escalating reciprocal disclosure modeled on Aron's 36 Questions framework, not free-form texting.

**CLICK (Chemistry)** — Four sub-mechanisms:

* **Admiration** — Seeing their unique value (confirmed from original thesis)

* **Self-Expansion** — Feeling like they expand your world (new)

* **I-Sharing** — Real-time shared subjective experience (new)

* **Arousal Transfer** — Novel/exciting shared context (new)

**COMFORT (Safety)** — Reducing performance anxiety so authenticity can emerge. A prerequisite for everything else to work.

**Core design principle:** Chemistry is not a trait people have. It is an experience that gets created by interaction design. The right app does not match people who have chemistry — it creates the conditions where chemistry can emerge between people who would otherwise never discover it.
